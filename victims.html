VICTIMS

WITNESSES

Rosetta Anderson: Rosetta Anderson claimed that on the evening of Wednesday, September 19th, 1888, she had encountered a "curious and mysterious man" on her doorstep.

J. Best and John Gardner: They had seen Elizabeth Stride in the company of a man in the doorway of the Bricklayers Arms, at a little before 11pm on the night of Saturday, September 29th, 1888. Best stated that he and his friends had been surprised by the way in which the man was "going on with the woman". He claimed that he was so suspicious of the man that, if he had seen a policeman, he would have had the man arrested.

James Brown: James Brown, a resident of Fairclough Street, went out to get some supper a little before a quarter-to-one, on the morning of Sunday, September 30th, 1888. As he made his way along Fairclough Street, he passed a man and woman standing by a wall. That woman turned out to be Elizabeth Stride.

Thomas Eade: Thomas Eade, a signalman, stated that on Saturday, September 8th, 1888, he was going down the Cambridge-road, towards the Whitechapel- road, when he saw a man who attracted his attention, as he was holding his arm stiffly. As he moved his arm, the witness saw the blade of a knife sticking out of his trousers pocket. Witness told some men of what he had seen, and then followed the man for some distance, intending to give him into custody, but he lost sight of him.

Elizabeth Long: At 5.30am on the morning of the 8th of September, 1888, she was on her way to work, and was walking along Hanbury Street. As she passed number 29 she saw a man and a woman talking together. She was later informed that the woman was Annie Chapman.

John Lardy: He approached the City Of London Police on the afternoon of Thursday, October 18th, 1888, and made a statement regarding a suspicious looking man who had the appearance of an American. Lardy and his friend had followed that man all over Whitechapel from 10.30pm on Wednesday, October 17th.

A young woman called Lyons: She stated that at three o'clock on Saturday afternoon she met a strange man in Flower and Dean Street, one of the worst streets in the East End of London. He asked her to go to Queen's Head public-house at half-past six and drink with him. While they were having a conversation, Lyons noticed a large knife in his right hand pants pocket, and called another woman's attention to the fact.

William Marshall: At around 11.45pm on Saturday, September 29th, 1888, William Marshall was standing outside his house at 64, Berner Street, when he saw a man and a woman standing on the pavement opposite. That woman turned out to be Elizabeth Stride.

Mrs. Paumier: Mrs. Paumier was selling roasted chestnuts in Widegate Street, a narrow thoroughfare that connects Sandys Row with Middlesex Street, when a very strange man, who was dressed like a gentleman, came up to her. He came to her and said, "I suppose you have heard about the murder in Dorset-street." She replied that she had, whereupon the man grinned, and said, "I know more about it than you." He then stared into her face and went down Sandys-row, a narrow thoroughfare which cuts across Widegate-street. When he had got some way off, however, he looked back as if to see whether she was watching him, and then vanished.

Sarah Roney: Sarah Roney, a girl of about twenty years of age, states that she was with two other girls on Thursday night in Brushfield Street, which is near Dorset street, when a man wearing a tall hat and a black coat, and carrying a black bag, came up to her, and said, "Will you come with me?" She told him that she would not, and asked him what he had in the bag, and he said, Something the ladies don't like. He then walked away.
